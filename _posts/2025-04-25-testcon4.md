On the 17th and 18th April 2025, I attended TestCon 4, the 4th edition of the annual testing conference in Mauritius.  It was organized by the Mauritian Software Testing Qualification Board (MSTQB) and featured speakers from both local and international entities, including the International Software Testing Qualification Board (ISTQB). 

It was my first time attending a testing conference - or any tech conference, for that matter. I had no professional experience with software testing and my knowledge was limited to writing unit tests for pet projects. Despite this, I went there with an open mind and curiosity about what the event could offer.

## Glossary from the Conference

During the two days spent at TechCon4 I came across a lot of new terms, as well as some I had simply forgotten about:

- **Equivalence partitioning**: Input data is divided into groups based on the expectation that the software will behave similarly for all inputs within a group (Wikipedia contributors, 2024).
- **Shift-left testing**: Approach in software development that emphasizes moving testing activities earlier in the development process (IBM, n.d).
- **Fuzz testing**: A technique that "pings" code with random inputs in an effort to crash it and thus identify faults that would otherwise not be apparent (Gitlab, n.d).
- **INVEST**:  It stands for Independent, Negotiable, Valuable, Estimable, Small, and Testable and is used to assess the quality of user stories.
- **BDD**: Behavior-driven development.
- **TMMi**: Test Maturity Model integration.
- **Gherkin syntax**: It is used for BDD and has 3 components: Given, When, Then.
- **Example mapping**: A method for determining the acceptance criteria of a user story before development starts.
- **RaaS**: Results As a Service which is an evolution of SaaS (Software as a Service).
- **RAG**: Retrieval Augmented Generation.
- **WCAG**: Web Content Accessibility Guidelines.
- **LCM**: Large Concept Models.
- **Copyleft**: Similar to copyright but mandates that derivative works be distributed under the same license.

## Artificial Intelligence

The overarching theme of the conference was Artificial Intelligence. Nearly all the talks revolved around the use of AI in software testing.

### Test Case Generation

Companies like MCB and Accenture are using AI to generate test cases and test data. For example, MCB developed a model capable of producing BDD tests. The advantage brought by AI is that it can generate a wider variety of test cases (positive, negative, and edge cases) in less time. Moreover, for security reasons, companies are training and hosting their own AI models. 

### Testing AI Agents

One interesting talk came from Rubric, a quality consulting company, who shared how they tested the output of an AI agent. Since AI is not non-deterministic and can hallucinate, that we need a way to evaluate their output. 

Rubric implemented an AI chatbot in a banking application and used the Ragas library (Exploding Gradients, n.d) to evaluate their AI. The ragas library provides a lot of metrics for assessing the performance of LLMs. However, this relies on a ground truth which can be hard-coded or come from another trusted AI.

Honestly, I did not see the value in testing an AI agent with the Ragas library. It felt like a chicken-and-egg problem: to evaluate the agent, you need a ground truth—but that "truth" is often generated by another AI. So how confident can we really be that this verifying agent is not just hallucinating too?

### AI-Driven Learning

<img src="werner-ai-learning.png" height ="446" width="702" alt = "Werner Henschelchen at TestCon 4">
_Werner Henschelchen at TestCon 4 (MSTQB, 2025)_

Werner Henschelchen, CEO of the ISTQB exam provider GASQ, gave a talk titled "AI-Driven Learning" where he showcased an AI tool designed to generate practice test questions. In an attempt to address the limited pool of practice questions available for the ISTQB certifications, Werner trained an AI model on the the syllabus, past paper questions, and their answers. No details were provided whether this was a fine-tuned model or one that was trained from scratch.

In addition, I was able to use the AI tool in question at an A4Q booth. After selecting a topic to practice, the web app gave me a question and a text box for an answer. Then after clicking on a verify button, the AI corrects my answer, gives me a score out of 100, and provides recommendations and a sample answer. Based on the 10 mins of practice that I had, the recommendations provided by the AI seemed decent. Using the AI recommendations, I created a feedback loop where I answered, verified my answer, and then updated it. I even attempted a prompt injection attack for testing purposes but failed.

<img src="ai-tool.png" height ="446" width="702" alt = "Web interface of the AI learning tool (Alliance 4 Qualification, 2024)">
_Web interface of the AI learning tool (Alliance 4 Qualification, 2024)_

While I do concede that AI can help in learning, as a complete beginner in software testing and based on my previous experience with LLMs, I do not think that this tool is enough on its own. The seemingly correct recommendations provided by the tool were not verified by a professional prior to being shown to me and I had no guarantee that the AI was not hallucinating while I was using it despite the reassurance from the booth operator.

The second day of the conference highlighted the limitations of artificial intelligence when it came to answering a question correctly for a goodies bag: "What are the four key areas of competence that define a person’s skillset?". Initially, several people simply fed the question to Google/AI on their phone and attempted to answer the question. However, after 10+ incorrect answers, the host revealed part of the answer which was "Personal competence". Finally, one participant managed to get the right answer by diving in the ISTQB’s syllabus: "Professional competence, Methodological competence, Social competence, and Personal competence" (ISTQB, 2024, p. 63).

### Can AI replace Testers

<img src="joel-oliviera.png" height ="446" width="702" alt = "Joel Oliviera at TestCon 4 (MSTQB, 2025)">
_Joel Oliviera at TestCon 4 (MSTQB, 2025)_

The talk titled "Yes, AI will replace you" by Joel Oliveira, founder of the Portuguese Software Testing and Qualifications Board and member of the ISTQB, was one of the most eye-opening talks of the conference. Despite the provocative and pessimistic title, Joel presented a plausible scenario where AI could displace software testers. The gist of his argument was as follows:

- The AI market is booming and AI is bound to get better with time.
- Software testers in developing countries are paid less than the same testers in developed countries. Low salaries are therefore a competitive advantage for testers in poorer countries which are leveraged to find jobs.
- If AI is introduced, the cost of hiring testers will increase and testers from poorer countries will lose their competitive advantage. Consequently there will be fewer testing jobs.
- Say there are 3 testers in a team. Now if 2 of the software testers learn to use AI in their tasks, the overall efficiency of the testing process will increase and the 3rd tester will no longer be required. Therefore Joel posits that software testers who cannot use AI will be replaced by AI.

Unfortunately, likely due to time constraints, Joel did not provide any actionable strategies for software testers to make the most of AI. The general advice to "learn to use AI" is not very useful for most attendees since many key questions remain unanswered, such as where to begin, how to juggle work and learning, and what resources to rely on.

Furthermore, I wondered whether that a similar argument can be made for the case that AI will replace software developers.

## Measuring the Efficiency of Testing Activities

<img src="eric-riou.png" height ="446" width="702" alt = "Eric Riou du Cosquer at TestCon 4 (MSTQB, 2025)">
_Eric Riou du Cosquer at TestCon 4 (MSTQB, 2025)_

Eric Riou du Cosquer, a TMMi Lead Assessor and CEO of Certilog, talked on how to measure the efficiency of your test activities. He recommended several metrics and supported them with personal experience:

1. Number (or percentage) of critical failures in production that could have been avoided by test activities.
2. Average criticality of defects detected during the first day/week. Here, the Prisma method is useful.
3. Number of defects detected in the most important input.
4. Average time elapsed between introduction of defect and its detection.
5. Average cost of testing per project with some level of quality.
6. Average percentage of false positives and false negatives after running tests. He stressed that false negatives are more important.
7. Average time spent by a tester in a company before leaving.

## Certifications & Training Providers

The ISTQB offers a wide range of internationally recognized certificates. If you want to get certified, the Certified Tester Foundation Level (CTFL) is your first target. Even though there are a lot certifications that can demonstrate your competency, an IT degree is still considered to be valuable.

<img src="gasq-portfolio.png" height ="446" width="500" alt = "ISTQB® Product Portfolio">
_ISTQB® Product Portfolio (GASQ, 2025)_

Here are some information regarding the Foundation course in the Mauritian context:

- Exams can be carried via remote proctoring or on-site in accredited exam centers.
- A4Q is responsible for authenticating the certifications, while Certilog is a training provider.
- The exam questions are multiple choice and scenario-based.
- Training costs Rs 28k and the exam itself costs Rs 8k. Certilog was offering a special 30% discount exclusively for TestCon attendees, valid until 31st April 2025.
- It is possible to skip the training phase and go directly to the exam.
- The Foundation certificate has no expiration date.

### Accessibility Testing Tools

1. **Deque** – A comprehensive suite for web accessibility testing.
2. **Wave** – A web accessibility evaluation tool that helps identify issues.
3. **Google Lighthouse** – A tool for for performing accessibility and performance checks.
4. **Funkify Chrome Extension** – A Chrome extension that simulates several disabilities.
5. **NVDA Screen Reader** – A free and open-source screen reader for Windows, widely used by visually impaired users.

## Some Honest Critiques

While the sessions were engaging, there were a few minor aspects that I did not like about the conference:

- The food, especially during lunch, did not meet expectations. The tea break snacks were too oily for my taste, and the lunches — dholl puri on the first day and a chicken-and-peas panini on the second — felt underwhelming. A balanced menu might have improve the overall experience.
- The slide decks were not made available to the public. Having access to it would’ve made it easier to review the key points.
- Surprisingly, there were very few students and lecturers present, despite the event being held in a university auditorium. This stood in stark contrast to the closing remarks by a university representative, who emphasized that the conference aimed to bridge the gap between academia and industry.

## Conclusion

My experience at TestCon 4 was positive. In the span of these two days I attended all 15 talks and interacted with a lot of people. It made me realize that the professional world of software testing is not only about writing unit tests. One highlight was receiving a book on software testing automation, personally autographed by the authors Eric Riou du Cosquer and Olivier Denoo — a nice souvenir from an inspiring event.
